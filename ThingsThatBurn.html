Things That Burn 2020

1. Hot Water

My t-shirt soaked. Instant noodle oozed on my chest. The neckline of my t-shirt had trapped the sluggish noodles, like a pasta spoon scooping up spaghetti. Some string slid through while some hung around.

A bowl shattered.

I maniacally clawed out the boiled noodles and rubbed and rubbed and rubbed.

“Why did you let your daughter eat Mama1?”, the paediatrician scorned my mother.

My mother did not let me eat Mama. I had eaten Mama because my mother had not been there to prevent me. She had not been home that fateful evening and I had been left under the care of my nannies.

When I was eight, I got to eat certain dishes only when my parents were both not home. Eating Yum Pla Kapong2 when they were at weddings and eating Mama when they were at funerals. Since my parents were rarely absent as a package, getting to eat these dishes was rare golden opportunistic moment.

That fateful night, I planned to eat Mama.

Just like with tea, there are different methods in preparing Mama. Most convenient store — 7-11, Family Mart, visitors use the infusion method: pressing hot water from the hot kettle, letting the noodles sink and steam in its industrial plastic container and its non-recyclable laminated foil cover, and waiting till the noodles soften.

That night, my nanny used the decoction3 method.

Decoction is the ancient Chinese way of making tea. It is to boil the tea — usually hard barks and herbs, on the stove over time. To bring water to a boil, nowadays, has a specific scientific meaning. The water reaches its boiling point at 100°C or (212°F). In ancient Chinese tea poems, however, the word “boil” was used more broadly and liberally and the temperature of “boiling” water varied.

Lu Yu 陆羽 (circa 733-804 A.D.), a Tang Dynasty4 Chinese Tea Master, vividly described three stages of boiling water in his book Chá Jīng 茶经 (Book of Tea, 780 A.D.).

《茶经》:“其沸如鱼目,微有声为一沸,缘边如涌泉连珠为二沸,腾波鼓浪为三沸,已上水老不可食也。”

The first sight of fish eyes, accompanied by whimpers is the first stage of boil.
When the edges turn into gushing springs and strings of pearls, it is the second.
When the water soared like tumbling drumming waves, it is the third.5

Like wise, Lü Dongbin (796-1016 A.D.) 呂洞賓, a Tang scholar and poet6, described a wave-like boiling water in his poem Dà Yún Sì Chá Shī 大雲寺茶詩 (Tea of Da Yun Temple). However, the waves were not preceded by fish eyes. Instead they were preceded by shrimp eyes.

《大云寺茶诗》:
蝦眼湯翻細浪俱

Shrimp eyes current turned
entirely into fine waves.

Later, during the Song dynasty, a calligrapher named Sushi 苏轼 (1037-1101 AD) described another stage of boiling water— xièyan 蟹眼 crab eyes7, in his poem Shì Yuàn Jiānchá 试院煎茶 (Trial Tea).

《试院煎茶》:
蟹眼已过鱼眼生
飕飕预作松风鸣

The crab eyes passed. Fish eyes are born.
Sou Sou, comes the cry of the freed wind.

Since the sizes of shrimp eyes and crab eyes are quite close to each other, Shrimp-Eyes-Boiling-Water must be slightly cooler if not the same temperature as Crab-Eyes-Boiling-Water. Based on the poem above and the fact that crab eyes are smaller than fish eyes, Crab-Eyes-Boiling-Water has a lower temperature than that of Fish-Eyes-Boiling-Water. Many ancient Chinese poets agreed and recorded that Crab-Eyes-Boiling-Water is the most suitable temperature of boiling water to use for tea decoction. The water must be kept only at the Crab-Eyes stage.

Since my nanny was preparing instant noodle not tea, the same delicacy was not deployed. She boiled the noodle in the water passed the crab eyes, the fish eyes, the gushing springs, into the tumbling waves. She knocked in an egg, broke in some Chinese cabbage and emptied out the pot into the serving bowl.

While I was walking from the kitchen to the dining room, holding that bowl of Mama sitting in the calming waves, I turned around to talk to my nanny and unknowingly poured the Mama and the waves onto myself.
